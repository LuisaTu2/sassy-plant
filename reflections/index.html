intro
Last summer I tried gardening at home for the first time. Armed with recycled coffee cups and cheap soil, I germinated store-bought seeds indoors, tracked frost dates and finally planted the surviving seedlings in my small yard in mid-spring.

I quickly realized that plants had their own care needs and in a way, showed different personalities. Tomatoes and marigolds almost craved a bit of neglect, while basil and roses would give me the middle leaf and dramatically wilt when the pH level wasn‚Äôt just right. Go figure.

I admire gardeners who find their hobby relaxing. If basil could talk, she would surely be a little cranky one, always complaining about something. So when I came across the voice AI hackathon opportunity, I thought: why not use AI to make a sassy plant talk to me?

the sassiest of plants
Meet the one and only, Lady Monstera Deliciosa! She is native to the tropical forests of southern Mexico, and is also known as the ‚ÄúSwiss cheese plant‚Äù due to the holes, or ‚Äúeyes,‚Äù that develop in her leaves, resembling the holes in Emmental cheese.

With her dramatic and "cheesy" deep green leaves, my recently adopted Monstera would make the perfect sassy talking plant!


tech details
Water and light are of course the basic needs for any plant to survive. I used a phototransistor and a soil moisture sensor to probe the plant's light and water levels. I connected both sensors to an arduino via a USB cable. Here is the schema:

The arduino sends data from the sensors to the backend via asynchronous serial communication in 1 second intervals. I used FastAPI (python) for the backend, React for the front-end and a combination of websockets and http for communication across the stack.

I am fairly new to websockets, so it was really cool implementing a two-way street communication channel between backend and frontend.

I used gpt-4o by OpenAI to handle text-to-speech (TTS) communication and give Lady Monstera her voice.

observations and next steps
  • from a technical perspective, I opted for text-to-speech instead of realtime speech. Realtime was very sensitive to ambient noise, difficult to control and it quickly became too expensive to play around with. I believe a small delay in response is perfectly acceptable for a talking plant.
  • it would be fun to gamify the experience, with multiple plants talking to each other, and implement different plant personalities based on each plant‚Äôs needs, and even connect hobbyist gardeners so that plants within a circle of friends could gossip about their owners ü§≠.
  • ultimately, I hope this project helps us get closer to our house plants and the nature around us, reduce house plant neglect and make interacting with our green friends a little more entertaining and curiosity driven.
